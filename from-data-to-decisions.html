From Data to Decisions: Research That Actually Ships

April.16.2026 · 4 min read

💡 A reflection on a guest lecture by Prakriti Parijat, Head of Research at Instacart

In academic settings, research often aims for precision, completeness, and rigor. In industry, those goals still matter—but they are constrained by something far more pressing: the need to make decisions quickly and effectively.

In a recent guest lecture, Dr. Prakriti Parijat offered a practical look at how research functions inside a fast-moving company like Instacart. Her message was clear:

Research does not exist to be perfect—it exists to inform decisions that move the product forward.

🧩 The Reality: No Perfect Sensor for Real Problems

One of the most grounding ideas from the talk was this:

There may be no perfect way to measure the problem you actually care about.

Whether it is user intent, decision-making, or behavior, our tools are always imperfect:

  • Surveys are biased
  • Behavioral data is incomplete
  • Sensors miss context

This forces a shift in mindset:

Instead of chasing perfect measurement, aim for useful understanding.

🔬How Research Fits Into Product Development

At Instacart, research is not a separate activity—it is embedded within the product lifecycle:

  • Shape strategy
  • Evaluate solutions
  • Measure impact
  • Scale customer intuition

Crucially, research plays a role from the very beginning:

  • Early problem definition
  • Opportunity identification

But an important boundary remains:

Research is an input to decisions—not the decision itself.

This distinction prevents over-reliance on any single method or dataset.

⚡ Speed vs. Rigor: The Industry Trade-off

In industry, rigor still matters—but it must coexist with speed.

  • You need just enough evidence to move forward
  • Waiting for perfect studies can delay progress

This leads to a pragmatic approach:

Aim for minimum viable evidence, not maximum certainty.

Deeper experiments are still valuable—but they come with:

  • Time costs
  • Resource constraints

So teams must constantly decide:

What level of rigor is enough for this decision?

💬 The Gap Between What People Say and What They Do

A classic challenge in user research:

People say they want healthy food—but they often choose what is familiar or convenient.

Why?

Because users rationalize their answers.

This creates a fundamental gap:

  • Stated behavior (what people say)
  • Actual behavior (what people do)

Understanding this gap is critical. It reinforces the need to:

Look at problems through multiple lenses, not just one dataset.

🔺 Triangulation for Population-Level Insight

To bridge these gaps, Dr. Parijat emphasized triangulation:

  • Combine surveys, behavioral data, and experiments
  • Interpret patterns across sources
  • Infer broader population intent

The goal is not to eliminate uncertainty—but to navigate it intelligently.

🗣️ Storytelling: The Most Underrated Skill

Even the best research can fail if it is not communicated effectively.

Without storytelling, the impact of research is lost.

Strong storytelling involves:

  • Visualization → making insights visible
  • Reframing → focusing on the problem, not just the solution
  • Human connection → bringing the customer into the room

This is what turns analysis into action.

🌫️ Thriving in Ambiguity

One of the most striking insights from the lecture:

The people who succeed are those who can sit with ambiguity.

In real-world product development:

  • There is rarely a clear path
  • Data is incomplete
  • Trade-offs are constant

This makes adaptability the most important meta-skill.

🤖 The Role—and Risk—of AI

AI can accelerate research in new ways:

  • Scanning social media
  • Identifying trends
  • Generating hypotheses

But there is a caveat:

The data behind AI is often biased toward certain audiences.

So while AI is powerful, it must be used critically and combined with other methods.

📈 Scaling Research as the Business Grows

As companies grow, so does complexity.

One strategy highlighted was:

Diversifying the research portfolio

This means:

  • Using multiple methods
  • Studying different user segments
  • Balancing short-term insights with long-term understanding

It ensures that research remains relevant across stages of growth.

✨ Final Thought

The biggest takeaway from this lecture was a shift in how we think about research:

Research is not about finding the “right answer.” It is about reducing uncertainty enough to act.

In fast-moving environments, success comes from:

  • Making informed decisions
  • Communicating insights clearly
  • Adapting as new information emerges

In the end, impactful research is not the most rigorous—it is the most useful.
