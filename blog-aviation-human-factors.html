Aviation Human Factors: Beyond the Lab, Into the Cockpit

April.88.2026 · 4 min read

✈️ A reflection on a guest lecture by Arjun Rao (Boeing)”

Human Factors Engineering (HFE) often looks clean and controlled in the lab. But in aviation, reality is far messier. In a recent guest lecture, Dr. Arjun Rao highlighted a critical gap: what works in the lab does not automatically translate to the flight deck.

The talk was a grounded look at how human factors is actually practiced in industry—where constraints, trust, and context matter just as much as data.

👀 When Sensors Meet Reality

Eye tracking is a powerful tool in research. But in aviation, even this “mature” technology faces practical challenges:

  • Sunlight saturation on the flight deck can disrupt measurements
  • Real-world environments introduce noise that labs cannot replicate

This raises an important point:

A method that works perfectly in controlled settings may fail in operational environments.

The implication is not to abandon such tools, but to understand their limits early and design with those constraints in mind.

🔒The Hidden Barrier: Trust

One of the most insightful points was about pilots’ concerns regarding data:

  • How is the data being used?
  • Will it be used for evaluation or surveillance?
  • Could it impact their careers?

Even if a method is technically sound, lack of trust can block its adoption.

This is where human factors goes beyond measurement:

You are not just studying humans—you are working with them.

🎤 Why Interviews Still Matter

In an era dominated by sensors and analytics, interviews might seem secondary. But Dr. Rao emphasized their importance:

  • Not just to validate findings
  • But to understand what is happening in the pilot’s mind

Flight operations are highly structured:

  • Procedures are well-defined
  • There is very little wiggle room in execution

So when deviations occur, they are meaningful. Interviews help uncover:

  • Intent
  • Interpretation
  • Context behind decisions

Without this layer, data can be misleading.

🏭The Reality of Industry Constraints

A recurring theme was the importance of time and resources.

In academic settings, running an experiment is often the default approach. But in aviation:

  • Getting pilots into experiments is difficult
  • Designing realistic scenarios is costly
  • Operational disruptions are not acceptable

This leads to a key realization:

Running an experiment is not always the answer in industry.

Instead of asking, “Which one is correct?”, the better question is:

“What story emerges when they disagree?”

⚖️Industry Reality: Actionable Consensus > Certainty

In practice, industry does not wait for perfect truth.

The goal is not certainty—it is actionable consensus.

A great example discussed was shoe design:

  • A shoe with “20% bounce” may perform well mechanically
  • But users may perceive it as uncomfortable or unstable

Now you have conflicting signals:

  • Sensors say “good”
  • Users say “bad”

What do you do?

Triangulation helps you decide:

  • Is the material wrong?
  • Or is the design/form causing the perception issue?

Further complexity emerges over time:

  • Foam loses elasticity
  • Performance changes with long-term use

This may lead to a different conclusion altogether:

→ Don’t fix the shoe—change the use case or target market

🧪Hypothesis-Driven Change (Not Data Fishing)

Another strong point was methodological:

Any change must be driven by a clear hypothesis, not random exploration.

Without this, triangulation becomes “data fishing”—looking for patterns without direction.

Instead:

  1. Form a hypothesis
  2. Use multiple data sources to test it
  3. Refine based on convergence or conflict

🌍When Triangulation Changes the World

History offers powerful examples where triangulation led to breakthroughs:

  • Three Mile Island accident → failure to integrate signals properly
  • Fosbury Flop → rethinking performance through new perspectives

These cases show that innovation often comes from reconciling conflicting views, not optimizing within a single one

🚧Barriers to Effective Triangulation

Despite its value, triangulation is difficult in practice. Two major challenges were highlighted:

  1. Lack of Bridging Frameworks

    2. Organizations often have data—but no structure to connect:

    • Sensors
    • User feedback
    • Observational insights
  2. Misaligned Constructs

Different teams define concepts differently:

  • “Fatigue” in physiology vs psychology
  • “Performance” in engineering vs user experience

When constructs don’t align, triangulation breaks down.

✨ Final Thought

The most important takeaway from the lecture was this:

Truth is not measured—it is negotiated across perspectives.

Designing systems, products, or experiments with this mindset forces us to move beyond single-metric optimization. It pushes us toward more robust, human-centered solutions.

In a world full of imperfect data and variable humans, triangulation is not just a method—it is a necessity.
