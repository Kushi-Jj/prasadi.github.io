Data Triangulation: Designing for Human Reality, Not Perfect Data

April.21.2026 · 4 min read

A reflection on a guest lecture by Prakriti Parijat, Head of Research at Instacart

In many technical fields, we are trained to believe that more data leads to better truth. But in a recent guest lecture, Dr. Rohith Karthikeyan challenged that assumption. There is no single source of truth—only converging perspectives.

This idea sits at the heart of data triangulation.

🌍 Seeing the World Through “Umwelt”

Dr. Karthikeyan began with the concept of Umwelt, a term describing how each organism experiences the world based on its sensory system.

Two key takeaways stood out:

  • Perception is not objective
  • Variation is not a flaw—it is an adaptation

What we perceive is shaped by our biology, context, and prior experiences. Even something as simple as viewing an object from the front versus the back can completely change interpretation. Importantly, this applies not just to humans, but also to sensors and systems.

In other words, data itself is not neutral—it is perspective-bound.

🔺Triangulation vs. Trilateration

A useful distinction from the lecture:

  • Trilateration → assumes a precise answer exists and tries to calculate it (like GPS)
  • Triangulation b>→ assumes no single source is fully reliable

Triangulation works by combining multiple viewpoints:

  • Different sensors
  • Different users
  • Different observers

The “truth” emerges at the intersection of these perspectives, not from any single one.

🎲 Humans Are Stochastic—and Products Must Reflect That

A particularly important idea was that humans are inherently stochastic. Our behavior is not fixed or deterministic.

Take fatigue as an example:

  • It varies by context
  • It adapts over time
  • t is influenced by personal bias

This means:

A good product is not one that assumes consistency—it is one that works well despite variability.

🔺The Triangle: Subjective, Objective, Observational

Dr. Karthikeyan described triangulation as balancing three types of data:

  • Subjective → what users report (feelings, perception)
  • Objective → measurable sensor data
  • Observational → what we see users actually do

A critical insight:

These three should be treated as independent sources, not forced to agree.

Instead of asking, “Which one is correct?”, the better question is:

“What story emerges when they disagree?”

⚖️Industry Reality: Actionable Consensus > Certainty

In practice, industry does not wait for perfect truth.

The goal is not certainty—it is actionable consensus.

A great example discussed was shoe design:

  • A shoe with “20% bounce” may perform well mechanically
  • But users may perceive it as uncomfortable or unstable

Now you have conflicting signals:

  • Sensors say “good”
  • Users say “bad”

What do you do?

Triangulation helps you decide:

  • Is the material wrong?
  • Or is the design/form causing the perception issue?

Further complexity emerges over time:

  • Foam loses elasticity
  • Performance changes with long-term use

This may lead to a different conclusion altogether:

→ Don’t fix the shoe—change the use case or target market

🧪Hypothesis-Driven Change (Not Data Fishing)

Another strong point was methodological:

Any change must be driven by a clear hypothesis, not random exploration.

Without this, triangulation becomes “data fishing”—looking for patterns without direction.

Instead:

  1. Form a hypothesis
  2. Use multiple data sources to test it
  3. Refine based on convergence or conflict

🌍When Triangulation Changes the World

History offers powerful examples where triangulation led to breakthroughs:

  • Three Mile Island accident → failure to integrate signals properly
  • Fosbury Flop → rethinking performance through new perspectives

These cases show that innovation often comes from reconciling conflicting views, not optimizing within a single one

🚧Barriers to Effective Triangulation

Despite its value, triangulation is difficult in practice. Two major challenges were highlighted:

  1. Lack of Bridging Frameworks

    2. Organizations often have data—but no structure to connect:

    • Sensors
    • User feedback
    • Observational insights
  2. Misaligned Constructs

Different teams define concepts differently:

  • “Fatigue” in physiology vs psychology
  • “Performance” in engineering vs user experience

When constructs don’t align, triangulation breaks down.

✨ Final Thought

The most important takeaway from the lecture was this:

Truth is not measured—it is negotiated across perspectives.

Designing systems, products, or experiments with this mindset forces us to move beyond single-metric optimization. It pushes us toward more robust, human-centered solutions.

In a world full of imperfect data and variable humans, triangulation is not just a method—it is a necessity.
